\begin{abstract}
\noindent We present an experimental implementation of the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) using classical optical hardware. Qubits are encoded in photonic polarization and spatial modes; single-qubit rotations and mode couplings are realized with wave plates, beam splitters and dove prisms, and readout is performed via Stokes-parameter measurements. We demonstrate the optical construction of a UCCSD-inspired two-qubit ansatz for H$_2$, report state-preparation fidelities from Stokes-based tomography, and discuss stability and scalability trade-offs for photonic VQE prototypes.
\end{abstract}
This work translates a small-molecule VQE workflow into an experimentally accessible optical testbed. The goal is not to claim quantum advantage, but to provide a transparent platform for prototyping variational circuits and measurement protocols using standard optics.

We implement qubits using two degrees of freedom of a single photon: polarization (horizontal $\ket{H}=\ket{0}_p$, vertical $\ket{V}=\ket{1}_p$) and a two-mode spatial basis (labels $\ket{0}_s,\ket{1}_s$). The main components are:
 Quarter-wave plates (QWP) and half-wave plates (HWP) for polarization rotations (Jones-matrix control).
 Non-polarizing and polarizing beam splitters (BS, PBS) for spatial-mode coupling and polarization routing.
 Dove prisms (DP) for controlled spatial-mode rotations. 

 Quantum \(R_x\) gate can be realized for polarization by using quarter-wave plate with fast axis at angle \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{4}\) with respect to the horizontal axis. 

\begin{equation}
 \text{QWP}(\frac{\pi}{4}) = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
		\cos^2 (\frac{\pi}{4}) + i \sin^2 (\frac{\pi}{4}) & (1-i) \sin(\frac{\pi}{4})\cos(\frac{\pi}{4})     \\
		(1-i) \sin(\frac{\pi}{4})\cos(\frac{\pi}{4})      & \sin^2 (\frac{\pi}{4}) + i \cos^2(\frac{\pi}{4})
	\end{bmatrix}
    = \frac{1+i}{2}
	\begin{bmatrix}
		1 & -i \\
		-i & 1
	\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
 Quantum $R_y(\frac{\pi}{2})$ realization in the optical circuit for Spatial Mode:
\begin{equation}
	\text{DP}(\frac{\pi}{4}) = \begin{bmatrix}
		\cos(\frac{\pi}{4}) & -\sin(\frac{\pi}{4}) \\
		\sin(\frac{\pi}{4}) & \cos(\frac{\pi}{4})
	\end{bmatrix} 
	= \begin{bmatrix}
		\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\
		\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
	\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
 Quantum $R_z(\theta)$ realization in the optical circuit for Polarization:
\begin{equation}
	\text{QWPh} \cdot \text{QWP}(\frac{-\pi}{4}) \cdot \text{HWP}(\frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{\phi}{4}) \cdot \text{QWP}(\frac{-\pi}{4}) \cdot \text{QWPh} = \begin{bmatrix}
	-ie^{-\frac{i\phi}{2}} & 0 \\
	0 & -ie^{\frac{i\phi}{2}}
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

Single-qubit rotations are mapped to Jones matrices realized with QWP/HWP sequences. For example, an $R_x(\pi/2)$ operation is produced with a QWP at $45^\circ$ (up to a global phase). Two-qubit interactions are emulated by coupling polarization and spatial degrees via PBS+BS networks: a polarization-controlled mode flip implements a classical-optical analogue of a CNOT between polarization and path.

The optical sequence for the two-qubit UCCSD-inspired ansatz used here is:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Prepare reference state $\ket{10}$ by applying a polarization flip on the appropriate spatial mode.
\item Apply parameterized single-qubit rotations on polarization and spatial-mode encodings using wave-plate angles $\{	\theta_i\}$.
\item Entangle via PBS/BS stage that routes polarization-dependent amplitudes between spatial modes.
\item Apply a phase gate on polarization implemented as a sequence of QWPs and HWPs (Jones-phase control).
\end{enumerate}
